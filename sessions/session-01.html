CASE FILE 01 / 24 — NUVORI LYCEUM

The Brilliant but Toxic Employee

Should a company keep an exceptionally productive employee whose behaviour damages colleagues and team culture?

One position. One challenge. One recommendation you'll have to defend live.

By the end of this session, you will be able to
  • I describe difficult workplace behaviour
  • II evaluate individual performance and team well-being
  • III explain professional consequences
  • IV make an HR recommendation
  • V suggest a fair intervention
  • VI defend and reconsider a position
Preparation

Watch, then reflect

Why Being Respectful to Your Coworkers Is Good for Business
SPEAKER: CHRISTINE PORATH · PLATFORM: TED

The talk explores how disrespectful behaviour affects employees, observers and organisations, and examines the connection between workplace respect, motivation and performance.

Preparation Questions

  1. Main Idea — What does the speaker want organisations to understand?
  2. Memorable Point — Which example, claim or finding stayed with you?
  3. Your Reaction — Which ideas did you agree or disagree with?
  4. Possible Limitation — Did the speaker simplify or overlook anything?
  5. Professional Connection — Can you connect the talk to a workplace situation you have observed? You do not need to mention names or confidential details.
The Dilemma

The Case of Elena

Elena is the strongest sales employee in a telecommunications company. She consistently exceeds her targets, retains important clients and generates a significant percentage of the department's revenue.

However, several employees have complained about her behaviour. She interrupts colleagues, publicly criticises junior staff, refuses to share information and occasionally takes credit for other people's work.

Two experienced employees have requested transfers because they find working with her exhausting.

Elena's manager has spoken to her informally twice. Elena insists that people are too sensitive. She believes that the company should value results rather than personalities.

Senior management is reluctant to take formal action because losing Elena could affect revenue and important client relationships. The HR department must now recommend what the company should do.

Your Initial Decision

Choose one position before class.

ARetain Elena without formal disciplinary action because her commercial value is too high.
BRetain Elena, but place her under a formal behavioural improvement plan.
CTransfer Elena to a more independent role with limited team contact.
DDismiss Elena because the wider organisational damage outweighs her individual results.
EPropose a different solution.

Be prepared to explain your decision and consider an opposing position.

Language Toolkit

Useful Language

1. A high-performing employee — an employee who consistently produces strong results.Elena is a high-performing employee, but her behaviour has become a serious concern.
2. Damage team morale — to reduce the motivation, confidence or unity of a team.Public criticism can gradually damage team morale.
3. Outweigh the benefits — to be more important or serious than the positive aspects.The long-term damage may outweigh the benefits of keeping her.
4. Tolerate unacceptable behaviour — to allow behaviour that should be challenged or stopped.Management should not tolerate unacceptable behaviour simply because an employee is successful.
5. Hold someone accountable — to require someone to accept responsibility for their behaviour or decisions.High-performing employees must also be held accountable for how they treat others.
6. Set a dangerous precedent — to create an example that may encourage similar behaviour in the future.Making an exception for Elena could set a dangerous precedent.
7. Undermine trust — to gradually weaken confidence between people.Taking credit for another person's work can undermine trust.
8. Address the behaviour directly — to raise and deal with a behavioural problem clearly and explicitly.The manager should address the behaviour directly.
9. A formal behavioural improvement plan — a documented process defining expected changes, support, review dates and possible consequences.Elena could be placed on a formal behavioural improvement plan.
10. Create a harmful working environment — to make a workplace feel threatening, disrespectful or psychologically unsafe. (Note: in some jurisdictions, "hostile work environment" is a specific legal term — worth knowing, not using casually.)Repeated humiliation can create a harmful working environment.
11. Retain valuable staff — to keep capable employees within an organisation.A healthy workplace culture helps companies retain valuable staff.
12. Take corrective action — to take steps intended to stop or repair a problem.Management must take corrective action before more employees leave.

Speaking Frames

Presenting a Balanced Position

I recognise that Elena brings considerable value to the company; however, …
Her performance should be taken into account, but it should not excuse …
The issue is not whether she is competent. The real issue is whether …

Discussing Consequences

In the short term, the company may benefit from keeping her, whereas in the long term …
The immediate loss of revenue must be weighed against …
What appears profitable individually may be damaging collectively.

Making a Recommendation

My initial recommendation would be to …
Before considering dismissal, I would first …
If she fails to modify her behaviour, the company should …

Expressing Caution

We should be careful not to label someone as toxic merely because …
One complaint may not be sufficient evidence; however, repeated patterns …
It is important to distinguish between a demanding employee and an abusive one.

Important Distinctions

Direct and Disrespectful — A direct employee communicates clearly and honestly. A disrespectful employee attacks, humiliates or dismisses other people.
Demanding and Abusive — A demanding manager expects high standards. An abusive manager uses intimidation, ridicule or threats.
A Difficult Personality and Misconduct — A difficult personality may be unpleasant or incompatible with others. Misconduct violates professional, ethical or organisational standards.
Conflict and Bullying — Conflict involves disagreement between people. Bullying usually involves repeated harmful behaviour and an imbalance of power.
An Isolated Mistake and a Behavioural Pattern — One incident may require clarification. Repeated behaviour may require formal intervention.

Grammar in Use

If the company ignores the complaints, more employees may resign.
If Elena is willing to cooperate, the company should offer her professional coaching.
If I were responsible for the case, I would place her on a formal improvement plan.
If management had acted earlier, the situation might not have escalated.
Discussion Map

What we'll explore

Elena's value to the company · the complaints against her · management's responsibility · the effect on the team · fairness and consistency · possible bias or misunderstanding · conflict-resolution strategies · formal and informal intervention · the conditions under which dismissal may become necessary.

The Conflict-Resolution Lens

  • The Parties Who is involved in the conflict?
  • Their Positions What does each side say it wants?
  • Their Interests What deeper concerns may exist beneath those positions?
  • The Evidence Which facts need to be verified?
  • The Intervention Could coaching, mediation or formal performance management help?
  • The Outcome What should happen if Elena refuses to change?
