The Brilliant but Toxic Employee

Should a company keep an exceptionally productive employee whose behaviour damages colleagues and team culture?

Quick Brief Talk Case Discussion Lens Decision Matrix Student Answers Language Final Task Assessment Notes
Teach Without Preparation

Emergency 3-Minute Brief

The talk in six sentences

Christine Porath studies incivility — rudeness and disrespect at work, from mocking and belittling to interrupting and excluding. Her research finds it doesn't just harm the target: witnesses lose focus and motivation too. Costs are measurable — people who experience or witness incivility cut back effort, get distracted, and are more likely to leave. Respect, by contrast, is built from small, repeatable acts — acknowledging people, listening fully, sharing credit. She uses Doug Conant's turnaround of Campbell's Soup as a case of a leader combining high standards with visible personal respect. Her closing question, "who do you want to be?", frames civility as a daily choice, not a personality trait.

The case in eight facts

1. Elena is the strongest sales performer in the company. 2. She generates a large share of departmental revenue and holds key client relationships. 3. Colleagues report interrupting, public criticism of juniors, withheld information, and credit-taking. 4. Two experienced employees have already requested transfers. 5. Elena's manager has raised it informally twice, with no change. 6. Elena's own view: colleagues are "too sensitive"; results should matter more than style. 7. Senior management is reluctant to act, fearing revenue and client loss. 8. No formal process, documentation or improvement plan exists yet.

The real tension

Whether individual commercial output can still count as "high performance" once the same person is reducing collective performance, trust and retention.

Likely defensible recommendation

Conduct a documented investigation, state behavioural expectations explicitly, place Elena on a time-bound behavioural improvement plan, protect complainants from retaliation, and dismiss her if serious misconduct is verified or improvement doesn't hold.

Strongest competing position

If evidence shows intimidation, retaliation, or repeated harm after prior informal warnings, a further improvement period may simply expose colleagues to more damage — the case for faster, firmer action.

Three non-negotiable distinctions

Directness vs disrespect · isolated incident vs repeated pattern · personality conflict vs misconduct. If a student's reasoning collapses one of these into the other, that's the moment to slow down and press.

Start Here If Class Begins Now

  1. Ask the learner for the talk's central idea.
  2. Clarify the Elena case in three questions.
  3. Obtain an initial recommendation.
  4. Challenge it with the strongest opposing argument.
  5. Move to the final recommendation.

If you only have 45 minutes

Brief opening (2 min) → three Primary Material questions (8 min) → Factual Grounding (5 min) → four CORE Deeper Discussion questions (15 min) → one Opposite-Position challenge (5 min) → Final Task (10 min).

If you only have 30 minutes

Case recap (2 min) → Initial Decision (3 min) → three CORE questions only (10 min) → Conflict-Resolution Lens, spoken through, not written (5 min) → one-minute recommendation (10 min).

Source Knowledge Bank

What the speaker actually argues

What Porath argues: incivility — rudeness, mocking, exclusion, interrupting — measurably damages effort, attention, creativity and retention, for the people who experience it and the people who witness it. Respect is not a soft add-on to performance; it's a driver of it.

Example she uses: Doug Conant's leadership at Campbell's Soup — turning around performance while being visibly, personally respectful (handwritten notes, attentive daily interactions), showing standards and civility aren't a trade-off.

What the lesson infers, not the talk itself: the Elena case is our own construction — Porath's talk doesn't discuss a "brilliant but toxic" employee directly. Don't let a student attribute the case's specifics to the speaker.

Reasonable limitation to raise if a student doesn't: the talk is framed around the *business* case for civility — critics might ask whether respect should need a commercial justification at all, or whether some roles (e.g. highly autonomous, individual-output roles) genuinely tolerate more friction than others.

Teacher-Safe Source Prompts

  • According to Porath, what happens not only to targets of incivility but also to witnesses?
  • Why does she treat respect as an organisational-performance issue, not just a moral one?
  • What does the Campbell's Soup example demonstrate?

Do not invent or quote exact percentages or statistics unless they are present in your own verified source notes — paraphrase the direction of the finding instead.

The Case

Factual Grounding — reference answers

Core What makes Elena valuable?
Reference answer

Exceptional sales results, key client relationships, and a significant share of departmental revenue.

Common omission

Students often say only "she works well" — press for the two distinct elements: measurable output and strategic client value.

Core What specific behaviours have caused concern?
Reference answer

Interrupting colleagues, publicly criticising junior staff, withholding information, taking credit for others' work.

Common omission

Students often generalise to "she's rude" without naming the specific behaviours — the specificity matters for a fair process.

Extend What evidence suggests the problem is serious?
Reference answer

Two experienced employees have already requested transfers — a costly, concrete outcome, not just complaints.

Core How has the manager responded so far?
Reference answer

Two informal conversations, no formal process, no documented expectations or consequences.

Common omission

Students often skip that the response has been informal only — this is exactly what makes a future dismissal procedurally risky.

Extend Why is senior management reluctant to act?
Reference answer

Fear of losing revenue and client relationships tied personally to Elena.

Core What risks does the company face if it dismisses her?
Reference answer

Revenue loss, client relationship disruption, and — since no formal warning exists — a real procedural fairness risk if she disputes it.

Core What risks does the company face if it keeps her?
Reference answer

Further transfers or resignations, a signal that results excuse conduct (a "dangerous precedent"), and continued erosion of trust in management.

Initial Position

What is your initial recommendation, and why?

Push immediately for the weakest point in the student's own position, not just their strongest reason — this is what the Opposite-Position Challenge later builds on.

Deeper Discussion

Performance, responsibility, fairness

Performance and Behaviour

Core Is professional behaviour part of job performance, or separate from it?
Indicative answer

Part of it — performance isn't only individual output, it's whether that output enables or obstructs collective results. If Elena's sales rise but turnover, silence and reduced team productivity follow, both sides of that ledger belong in her evaluation.

Acceptable alternative

In highly individual roles, output and conduct may be scored separately, provided misconduct is still managed seriously — separation can aid transparency but must not excuse harm.

Strong answer notices
  • expands the definition of "performance" beyond output
  • names both measurable and hidden costs
  • avoids a purely moral conclusion with no organisational reasoning
Language to listen for

outweigh the benefits · take corrective action

Follow-up if shallow

"What would you actually measure to prove team productivity dropped because of her?"

Weak-answer sign

"She's rude, so she's not a good employee" — a conclusion with no organisational reasoning behind it.

Core Can someone be called a high performer if colleagues become less productive because of them?
Indicative answer

Only under a narrow, individual-output definition of "high performer." Widen the lens to the team, and a person whose presence measurably lowers everyone else's output is a net negative even if their own numbers look strong.

Acceptable alternative

In roles with genuinely minimal team interdependence, individual output may fairly stand alone — the question is whether Elena's role is actually that isolated, and the case suggests it isn't.

Strong answer notices
  • names which definition of "performance" they're using and why
  • tests whether Elena's role is genuinely independent or not

Extend Should team impact be included in performance evaluation?
Answer direction

Yes in most modern HR frameworks, though measurement difficulty is a fair counterpoint worth naming, not dismissing.

Key distinction

Measurable output vs. harder-to-quantify team cost — the second is real even when it's harder to put a number on.

Follow-up

"How would HR actually measure that, in practice?"

Extend Is financial performance easier to measure than cultural damage?
Answer direction

Yes — which is precisely why cultural damage gets under-weighted in real decisions, a good bridge back to the central tension.

Key distinction

Easy to measure vs. important to measure — the two are not the same thing.

Follow-up

"If it's hard to measure, does that make it less real?"

Extend Why do companies often tolerate harmful behaviour from strong performers?
Answer direction

Short-term visible cost (losing revenue) outweighs long-term invisible cost (culture, turnover) in most decision-makers' thinking.

Key distinction

Visible, immediate cost vs. invisible, delayed cost.

Follow-up

"What would make the invisible cost visible enough to act on?"

Indicative answer

Acceptable alternative

Some argue individual accountability shouldn't be diluted by pointing at management — Elena still chose the behaviour regardless of what management did or didn't do.

Extend At what point does tolerance become approval?
Answer direction

Roughly when a pattern repeats after being raised without any change in consequence — silence starts to read as consent.

Key distinction

A single delay vs. a pattern of delay with no follow-through.

Follow-up

"How many repeats before silence becomes a decision?"

Extend Are managers partly responsible when harmful employees gain influence?
Answer direction

Yes, especially where a formal process was available and simply went unused.

Key distinction

No process existing vs. a process existing but not being used.

Follow-up

"Was the process missing, or just not used?"

Extend Should colleagues confront Elena directly?
Answer direction

Peer-level confrontation is risky without organisational backing; better routed through a documented process.

Key distinction

Peer feedback vs. a formal complaint with organisational weight behind it.

Follow-up

"What protects a colleague who raises this directly?"

Fairness

Core Would dismissal be fair if expectations had never been stated clearly?
Indicative answer

Procedurally risky — without documented expectations, a dismissal is harder to defend if challenged. This is exactly why a formal improvement plan should precede dismissal in this case, not skip straight to it.

Acceptable alternative

If misconduct is severe enough (harassment, retaliation), some organisations argue immediate action is still justified regardless of prior documentation.

Strong answer notices
  • separates procedural fairness from the underlying merits
  • names what documentation would resolve the risk
Follow-up if shallow

"What would you need on file before you'd feel the dismissal was defensible?"

Core How should HR distinguish genuine misconduct from personality conflict?
Indicative answer

Three markers: a repeated pattern rather than a single clash, corroboration across multiple independent reporters, and evidence of a power imbalance rather than a mutual disagreement.

Acceptable alternative

Some argue intent matters too — but intent is hard to verify, so pattern and corroboration remain the safer, evidence-based test.

Strong answer notices
  • gives testable criteria, not just a feeling
  • notices that multiple independent reports outweigh a single account

Extend Should the same behavioural standards apply to average and exceptional employees?
Answer direction

Yes in principle; in practice this is precisely where "dangerous precedent" language becomes useful.

Key distinction

Standards that flex vs. standards that hold regardless of output.

Follow-up

"What message does a flexed standard send to everyone else?"

Extend Is it reasonable to give a valuable employee more opportunities to improve?
Answer direction

Reasonable once, risky if it becomes indefinite — time-bounding the plan is the key safeguard.

Key distinction

A bounded second chance vs. an open-ended one.

Follow-up

"How many chances is fair, and who decides?"

Extend Could some complaints reflect jealousy or resentment?
Answer direction

Possible in isolation, unlikely when multiple independent colleagues report the same specific behaviours.

One account vs. corroborated, independent accounts.

Discussion Map Guidance

Conflict-Resolution Lens, walked through

Parties

Teacher explanation: get every stakeholder named before opinions start, or the discussion collapses into "Elena vs everyone."

Case-specific answer: Elena · affected colleagues · Elena's manager · senior management · HR · clients.

Likely student confusion: forgetting clients as a party — they're indirect but shape senior management's reluctance.

Positions

Case-specific answer: Elena wants to be judged on results alone; colleagues want the behaviour to stop; management wants the problem to disappear without cost; HR wants a defensible, documented process.

Likely student confusion: treating "position" and "interest" as the same thing — that's exactly what the next panel corrects.

Underlying Interests

Teacher explanation: a position is what someone says they want; an interest is why. Two stated positions can conflict while their interests don't.

Case-specific answer: Position "Elena must be dismissed" → Interest: the team needs respect and psychological safety. Position "Elena must remain" → Interest: the company wants to protect revenue and client relationships.

Decision consequence: a plan that protects both interests (safety and revenue) — a time-bound improvement plan — usually beats either extreme.

Evidence

Teacher reference: documented complaints (dates, language, witnesses) · previous informal feedback · turnover/transfer requests · team performance changes · client dependence · Elena's own account · any sign of retaliation · consistency across independent reports.

Caution: anonymous complaints protect employees initially, but a formal process must still give Elena enough information to understand and answer the allegations fairly.

Intervention

Case-specific answer: direct feedback · formal warning · mediation · behavioural coaching · performance improvement plan · transfer · monitoring period · dismissal — in roughly ascending order of severity.

Likely student confusion: jumping straight to dismissal without naming the intermediate steps a fair process requires first.

Outcome

Case-specific answer: successful improvement looks like verified behaviour change within a stated window; failure triggers the next-severity intervention, decided in advance, not improvised.

Decision consequence: deciding the "what if it doesn't work" step now — before the conversation happens — is what makes the plan defensible rather than indefinite.

Opposite-Position Challenge

Defend the other side

After the learner defends one position, ask them to defend the opposite one. Possible roles: Elena · a junior employee she has criticised · the commercial director · the HR manager · the chief executive · a colleague who believes the complaints are exaggerated.

For the next two minutes, defend the position you disagree with most.

Purpose: develop flexibility · prevent rehearsed answers · strengthen concession and counterargument · reveal gaps in reasoning.

Common Student Answers

What to do with typical positions

"Fire her immediately. Toxic people never change."
  1. Validate the concern
  2. Ask what evidence supports dismissal
  3. Introduce procedural fairness
  4. Ask whether expectations were previously stated
  5. Challenge the absolute word "never"

Suggested prompt: "Your concern about protecting the team is clear. What process would make the dismissal fair and defensible?"

"Keep her — the revenue matters too much to lose."
  1. Acknowledge the real commercial risk
  2. Ask what it's costing the team already
  3. Introduce the "dangerous precedent" concept
  4. Ask what happens if a second Elena appears next year
"Just warn her once and see what happens."
  1. Ask what happens if nothing changes
  2. Point out two informal warnings have already failed
  3. Push toward a time-bound, documented plan instead
"Transfer her somewhere with less team contact."
  1. Ask whether this fixes or just relocates the problem
  2. Ask how the receiving team would be protected
  3. Check whether her role realistically allows this
"This is management's fault, not Elena's."
  1. Agree management contributed via inaction
  2. Ask whether that removes Elena's own responsibility
  3. Push for a both/and answer, not either/or
"Maybe the complaints are just jealousy."
  1. Ask how many independent colleagues have complained
  2. Introduce the corroboration/pattern test
  3. Ask what would change their mind if it isn't jealousy
"I don't know — it depends." (avoids taking a position)
  1. Accept genuine complexity as valid
  2. Ask what single new fact would resolve it for them
  3. Require a provisional position "for today," reversible later
Language Toolkit Activation

Target expressions and correction bank

Do not force all of them. Selectively prompt: "Could you express that using 'outweigh the benefits'?"

Private Upgrade Expressions

Flight risks — valuable employees who may realistically leave if conditions don't improve. "Two experienced employees are now considered flight risks."

Enable the behaviour — to make someone's harmful conduct possible or easier by failing to respond to it. "Management's silence has, in effect, enabled the behaviour."

Offer only if the student is already comfortable with the twelve target expressions. Introduce as better English, never as a labelled tier — in print or aloud.

Final Speaking Task

Three calibrated models

You are presenting your recommendation to senior management. You have approximately two minutes.

Model A — Competent B2

~140 words

My recommendation would be to place Elena on a formal improvement plan. She brings a lot of revenue, but her behaviour is a real problem for the team. Two people have already asked to transfer, so this is serious. The company should tell her clearly what needs to change, and give her a fixed amount of time to change it. HR should also protect the people who have already complained. The risk is that Elena might not take it seriously because she thinks her results protect her. But if we do nothing, more good employees might leave. If she doesn't improve, I think the company should dismiss her, because the damage to the team is more important than the money she brings in.

Clear position, understandable reasons, basic linking ("but," "so," "if"). Limited hedging, limited nuance — states things as fact rather than qualifying them.
Why it meets the benchmarkRecommendation (opening line) · Evidence (two transfer requests) · Immediate action (formal plan) · Risk (Elena may not take it seriously) · Conclusion (dismissal if no change) — moves are present but plainly stated, no concession.

Model B — Strong B2+/C1

~210 words

My recommendation would be to place Elena on a time-bound behavioural improvement plan, although I recognise that a more immediate response might feel more satisfying to the team. Elena generates a significant share of departmental revenue, but two informal conversations have already failed to change her behaviour, and two experienced colleagues have requested transfers as a direct result — that's no longer a personality issue, it's a pattern with a measurable cost. The company should first document the specific behaviours and set explicit, time-bound expectations, while HR ensures the employees who've already come forward are protected from any retaliation. The strongest argument against this approach is that another improvement period, without stronger safeguards, may simply expose colleagues to further harm — and I can't dismiss that concern easily. That's why I'd attach visible senior backing to the plan, not just an HR memo, so it isn't seen as optional. If verified improvement doesn't hold within the agreed window, dismissal should follow, because at that point the cost of tolerating the behaviour outweighs the revenue it protects. Ultimately, the deciding factor for me is that performance has to include how someone's presence affects everyone else's ability to perform.

Hedged claims ("although I recognise," "may simply"), explicit concession before rebuttal, names a mechanism (pattern, safeguards) rather than just an opinion, closes on a stated decision principle.
Why it meets the benchmarkRecommendation (early, hedged) · Concession (the retaliation-risk counter-argument, named explicitly) · Evidence (pattern across colleagues, two failed informal talks) · Immediate action (documented plan + senior backing) · Risk (named in her own recommendation, not just the alternative) · Conclusion (states the deciding principle).

Model C — Alternative Defensible Position

~180 words

My recommendation would be to dismiss Elena now, although I recognise this is the harder-line option. The pattern here isn't one bad day — it's repeated behaviour across multiple colleagues, unchanged after two direct conversations, and it has already cost the company two experienced employees. The strongest argument against immediate dismissal is procedural fairness, since no formal warning has been documented — and that's a real risk I wouldn't ignore. That's why I'd move quickly to a short, formal process rather than skip it entirely: document the pattern properly this week, give Elena a brief, clearly bounded chance to respond to specific allegations, and proceed to dismissal if the facts hold up, which based on the evidence so far, I expect they will. Waiting longer under an open-ended improvement plan risks losing more of the team while we wait to see if she changes. Ultimately, the deciding factor for me is that the company has already given her two informal chances, and a third one is a cost the team shouldn't have to keep absorbing.

Proves Option D can meet the same benchmark as Option B — same moves (recommendation, concession, mechanism, conclusion), different conclusion. Use this if a student defends dismissal and you're tempted to mark it down for "wrong answer" — it isn't one.
Why it meets the benchmarkRecommendation (early, hedged as "the harder-line option") · Concession (procedural fairness risk, named directly) · Evidence (repeated pattern, two failed conversations, two departures) · Immediate action (short formal process this week) · Risk (implicit in the concession) · Conclusion (states the deciding principle).
